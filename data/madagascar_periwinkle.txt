Title: Madagascar Periwinkle
Source: Kew
URL: https://www.kew.org/plants/madagascar-periwinkle

Scientific name: Catharanthus roseus
Common name: Madagascar periwinkle
Family: Apocynaceae

Description:
Madagascar periwinkle is a herb or small shrub that can sprawl along the ground or grow upright. It has dark green oval leaves and pink or white flowers with five petals.
The Madagascar periwinkle is valued for both its beauty and medicinal properties.
Along with being grown as an ornamental, the plant is also vital in the production of anti-cancer medications.
Originally only found on the eponymous island, the Madagascar periwinkle is now widely cultivated around the world.
Despite many of Madagascar’s native species being under threat from human activity, the periwinkle has continued to thrive, thanks to its affinity for growing in disturbed areas.
All parts of the Madagascar periwinkle are toxic if eaten. The medicinal compounds must be isolated to become effective treatments.
Madagascar periwinkle grows as a herb or small shrub, sprawling along the ground or growing up to 1m tall. The flowers can be pink or white, with five petals and a long tube connecting them to the stem. The leaves are dark green, oval shaped, and grow in opposite pairs along the stem. The fruits are made up of two narrow, cocoon-like pods, which dry out and open to reveal a number of black, grooved seeds.

Plant uses:
1. Ornamental:
- Commonly grown as a decorative flowering plant in warm regions.

2. Cultural:
- Madagascar periwinkle is a popular ornamental plant for growing in pots or beds.

2. Health:
- Known as the source of important anti-cancer compounds.
- Associated with medicines used in cancer treatment.
- Madagascar periwinkle is a source of anti-cancer medicines vinblastine, vincristine and vinorelbine.
- Madagascar periwinkle, and other Catharanthus species, are used in both Ayurvedic and traditional Chinese medicine as treatments for diabetes, hypertension and leukemia.
- A medicine that increases insulin secretions, vincoline, has been extracted from Madagascar periwinkle.

Warning:
- All parts of the plant are toxic if eaten.
- Medicinal compounds must be isolated and used safely.

Seasonality:
Best time to see:
- Flowers: Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug
- Fruits: Sep, Oct
- Foliage: Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov, Dec

Growing conditions:
- Best suited to warm climates.
- Can be cultivated widely in tropical and subtropical regions.
- Tropical and subtropical areas, on sand and limestone soils in woodland, forest, grassland, and disturbed areas.

Distribution:
- Native range: eastern and southern Madagascar.
- Introduced: Alabama, Aldabra, Andaman Is., Angola, Argentina Northeast, Aruba, Ascension, Assam, Bahamas, Bangladesh, Belize, Benin, Borneo, Burkina, California, Cambodia, Cameroon, Canary Is., Cape Provinces, Caroline Is., Cayman Is., Central American Pac, Chad, Chagos Archipelago, China South-Central, China Southeast, Christmas I., Comoros, Cook Is., Costa Rica, Cuba, Cyprus, Djibouti, Dominican Republic, East Himalaya, Easter Is., El Salvador, Eritrea, Ethiopia, Fiji, Florida, French Guiana, Gabon, Galápagos, Gambia, Gilbert Is., Guatemala, Guinea, Gulf of Guinea Is., Haiti, Hawaii, Honduras, India, Italy, Ivory Coast, Jamaica, Jawa, Kazan-Retro

Notes:
Madagascar periwinkle is one of the best-known medicinal plants because of its importance in cancer treatment research and medicine.
The genus name of the Madagascar periwinkle, Catharanthus, is Greek for 'pure flower'.
Along with their medical importance, the periwinkle is also used as a host plant to study unique plant bacteria called phytoplasmas.
