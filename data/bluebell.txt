Title: Bluebell
Source: Kew
URL: https://www.kew.org/plants/bluebell

Scientific name: Hyacinthoides non-scripta
Common name: Bluebell
Family: Asparagaceae

Description:
Bluebells bursting into bloom are a sure sign of spring. The bluebell is a herb that grows from a bulb. It has linear leaves and a flowering stem that grows up to 50 cm tall and droops to one side. The sweet-scented, nodding heads of flowers are bell-shaped and can be violet-blue and sometimes white or pastel pink. Bluebell is a spring-flowering woodland plant known for its delicate, nodding blue-violet flowers. It is one of the most recognizable wildflowers in parts of Europe and is especially associated with woodlands. 

Plant uses
1. Beauty and cosmetics:
	- Bluebell-inspired scents are added to soaps and hand creams.
2. Cultural:
	- Bluebells make stunning ornamental displays in woodland or semi-shaded gardens, among trees or in herbaceous borders.
3. Health:
	- The bulb of bluebells has been used in traditional medicine as a diuretic (increases urination) or styptic (stops bleeding).
	- Bluebells are not used in modern medicine due to their toxicity; they contain glycosides that are poisonous, and their sap can cause contact dermatitis.
	- Bluebell bulbs were crushed to provide starch for the ruffs of Elizabethan collars and sleeves.
Bluebells are mainly valued as ornamental and wildflowers. They are appreciated for their beauty, their role in spring displays, and their ecological importance in natural habitats.

Plant characteristics:
Bluebells usually produce narrow leaves and flowering stems with drooping, bell-shaped flowers. They are often found growing in colonies and can create dense carpets of blue in woodland areas during the flowering season.

Growing conditions:
Bluebells generally prefer moist but well-drained soil and are often associated with partial shade or woodland environments. They grow especially well in naturalistic garden settings that resemble their native habitat.

Seasonality:
Bluebells are especially associated with spring (April, May) and are best known for their spring flowering display.

Distribution
Where in the world:
	- Native: Belgium, France, Great Britain, Ireland, Netherlands, Portugal, Spain
	- Introduced: Austria, British Columbia, Falkland Islands, Germany, Indiana, Italy, Kentucky, New York, New Zealand North, New Zealand South, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Romania, Virginia, Washington
