Title: Common Snowdrop
Source: Kew
URL: https://www.kew.org/plants/common-snowdrop

Scientific name: Galanthus nivalis
Common name: Common snowdrop
Family: Amaryllidaceae

Description:
Common snowdrop is a bulbous plant that often grows in clumps. It has smooth, narrow, grayish-green leaves and nodding white flowers. Each flower appears at the end of a single green, leafless stalk.

Plant uses:
1. Cultural:
- Common snowdrop is one of the most popular cultivated bulbous plants in the world.
- It is commonly grown as an ornamental plant in parks and gardens.
- It has become naturalized in the UK and in several other European countries and the United States.

2. Health:
- Snowdrops contain galantamine, a compound used in modern pharmaceuticals to help manage Alzheimer’s disease.
- Galantamine is also used to relieve traumatic injuries to the nervous system.
- A lectin in common snowdrops called GNA is being studied for possible use against HIV.
- The same lectin has been described as an effective insecticide against several pests.

Warning:
- Common snowdrop should not be eaten.
- It contains lycorine, a poisonous compound that can cause diarrhea, nausea, and vomiting.

Did you know?
- Common snowdrop belongs to the same family as daffodils.
- Its seeds are dispersed by ants.
- The scientific name Galanthus nivalis means “milk flower of the snow”.

Growing conditions:
- Common snowdrop grows in woodland, grassland, and shrubland.
- It prefers well-drained chalk and limestone soils.
- It often grows under the shade of deciduous trees.

Seasonality:
Best time to see flowers: January, February, March.

Distribution:
Native: Albania, Austria, Belarus, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Bulgaria, Croatia, Czech Republic, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Italy, Kosovo, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Poland, Romania, Serbia, Sicily, Slovakia, Slovenia, Spain, Switzerland, Türkiye-in-Europe, Ukraine.

Introduced: Belgium, Great Britain, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Netherlands, New Brunswick, New Jersey, New York, Newfoundland, North Carolina, Norway, Ohio, Ontario, Pennsylvania, Sweden, Utah, Virginia, Washington.

Conservation:
- IUCN Red List status: Near Threatened.
- Common snowdrops are threatened in the wild by habitat destruction and over-collecting for the horticultural trade.
- All snowdrops (Galanthus spp.) are listed in CITES Appendix II, so trade is monitored and regulated.

Notes:
Common snowdrop is an important early spring flower known for its delicate white blooms, ornamental value, and conservation relevance.
